# Why Your Boss Trusts the Incompetent Guy Over You — Han Feizi Figured This Out in 233 BC

You do great work. You hit every deadline. Your code is clean. Your slides are perfect.

But the guy who does average work — the one who always volunteers first when the boss proposes something, who somehow always ends up in the boss's office — he just got promoted.

You think: "The boss must be blind."

Han Feizi would tell you: the boss isn't blind. You are.

---

## You're Competing on Competence. He's Competing on Trust.

Han Feizi, writing in the 3rd century BC, laid out a theory of power that is still the most honest thing ever written about how organizations actually work.

His core insight: **"The ruler's greatest danger lies in trusting people. Trust someone, and you are controlled by them."**

This isn't paranoia. It's a structural reality of any hierarchy. Every time a boss delegates power, they open a vulnerability. The person they delegate to now has information the boss doesn't have. They have relationships the boss can't see. They could be building something behind the boss's back.

So who does the boss promote? Not the most competent person. The person the boss believes WON'T use that vulnerability against them.

You show up with great work. That proves you're competent. It does not prove you're loyal. The average guy who always volunteers first, always supports the boss in meetings, always makes sure his work is VISIBLE to the boss — he's not showing competence. He's showing a signal: **"Your interests are my interests."**

That signal is worth more than your competence. Because competence without trust is a threat. Trust without competence is at worst a neutral employee who won't hurt you.

Han Feizi called this **"术" (shù)** — the art of management. The boss's primary job is not to find the best worker. It's to observe who is genuinely aligned with him and who is just performing alignment. The incompetent guy who is genuinely aligned will be promoted over the competent guy who is merely performing competence.

---

## What To Do About It — Without Becoming Someone You Hate

You don't need to become a sycophant. Han Feizi was executed by his own classmate — he knew better than anyone that playing the game doesn't guarantee winning it.

But you can do three things:

**Make your work visible to the boss yourself.** Don't assume "good work speaks for itself." It doesn't. The boss has 30 direct reports. He sees maybe 10% of what each person does. If you don't show him, someone else will show him THEIR work. Not because they're better — because they understood that visibility is part of the job.

**When the boss proposes something, respond first.** Not "suck up" — just be the first person in the room to say "I'll take a look at this and get back to you with a plan." You're not agreeing blindly. You're signaling: I'm on your team. I'll figure out the details. You can count on me to move things forward.

**Give the boss information he can't get elsewhere.** Han Feizi said the ruler's greatest vulnerability is information asymmetry — the people beneath him know things he doesn't. If you're the one person who voluntarily briefs the boss on what's actually happening on the ground — not gossip, not snitching, but honest situational awareness — you become irreplaceable. Not because you're the smartest. Because you're the one person who reduces his vulnerability instead of exploiting it.

---

Han Feizi died in a Qin dynasty prison cell, poisoned by his own classmate Li Si, who couldn't stand having someone smarter than him near the king. He knew the rules of power better than anyone. He just couldn't save himself from them.

You don't need to become Han Feizi. You just need to understand: your boss isn't rewarding incompetence. He's rewarding **predictable loyalty**. If you can be both competent AND predictable in your loyalty — you're unstoppable.
